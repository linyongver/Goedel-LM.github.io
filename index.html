Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive capabilities in reasoning, particularly in solving mathematical problems. However, relying solely on natural language for reasoning poses challenges in verification, undermining trust in practical applications. Formal theorem proving offers a promising solution: training LLMs to generate proofs in formal languages that can be mechanically verified. A key obstacle in this domain is the scarcity of formal data. To address this challenge, we begin by training formalizers to transform extensive datasets of natural language problems into formal statements. Next, we train the prover through a standard iterative process that involves generating and verifying proofs based on these statements, followed by training the prover using the verified proofs. Our model outperforms the previous state-of-the-art whole-proof generation model, DeepSeek-Prover-v1.5-RL, by 7.6\% on the miniF2F benchmark, achieving a Pass@32 score of 57.6\%. Furthermore, we have cumulatively solved 29.7K problems in Lean Workbook, significantly increasing the existing 16K proofs found by InternLM2.5-StepProver and InternLM-Math-Plus. We conduct thorough evaluations across several datasets and contribute open-source resources to support future research in formal theorem proving.

Methodology

A comprehensive approach to automated theorem proving through large-scale data synthesis and iterative training

Statement Formalization
  • Formalizer A: Trained on Lean workbook pairs
  • Formalizer B: Trained on 170K Claude-formalized statements
  • Each problem receives multiple formalizations

Detailed Process

We train two distinct formalizers to enhance statement diversity:

Formalizer A: Trained using Qwen2.5-Coder-32B on natural language and formal language pairs from Lean workbook.

Formalizer B: Utilizes Claude-sonnet-3.5 to formalize 230K statements, with 170K successfully passing compilation.

Training completed in under 24 hours using 8 H100 GPUs. Each problem receives 16 total formalizations (8 from each formalizer).

Quality Assessment
  • CC Test: Verifies syntax and compilation
  • FC Test: Verifies accuracy and completeness
  • Minimum FC score threshold of 0.5

Compiling Correctness (CC) Test

  • Ensures formalized statements follow Lean syntax
  • Must successfully compile with placeholder ":= by sorry"

Faithfulness and Completeness (FC) Test

  • Evaluates accuracy of problem translation
  • Uses Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct for assessment
  • Generates 4 independent judgments per statement
  • Statements below 0.5 FC score are filtered out
Iterative Training
  1. Generate 16 proof candidates
  2. Verify correctness
  3. Collect verified proofs
  4. Train new model
  5. Repeat process

Training Process

  • Uses DeepSeek-Prover-V1.5-RL for initial proof generation
  • Generates 16 proofs per problem
  • Compiles proofs to verify correctness
  • Retains one correct proof per statement
  • Trains new model (Prover-Iter-k) based on collected proofs
  • Iteratively improves through multiple training cycles

Training Parameters

  • Learning rates: 1e-4 and 5e-5
  • Training epochs: 1-2
Data Scale
  • 928K informal statements processed
  • 760K dual-formalization statements
  • 123K single-formalization statements
  • 140K Leanworkbook statements
  • Total: 1.78M formal statements

Data Sources

  • 860K problems from Numina datasets
  • 68K private collection from Art of Problem Solving (AOPS)
  • 140K statements from Leanworkbook and Leanworkbook-plus

Formalization Results

  • 760K problems with dual formalizations (both Formalizer A and B)
  • 123K problems with single formalization
  • Comprehensive coverage across different mathematical domains

Experimental Results

Benchmarks

Following previous works, we primarily use miniF2F as our main evaluation benchmark. Additionally, we track performance on ProofNet, Lean-workbook, and our held-out evaluation dataset. The benchmarks represent a diverse range of mathematical problems, from high-school level to undergraduate mathematics.

Key Findings

  • Goedel-Prover-SFT achieves state-of-the-art performance of 57.6% on miniF2F at Pass@32
  • Significant improvements across multiple datasets with an average performance gain of 5.4%
  • Best performance on PutnamBench with 8 problems solved out of 644
  • Using both formalizers (A and B) leads to better performance compared to single formalizer approaches
  • ProofNet performance shows different characteristics compared to other datasets
  • Iterative training process demonstrates consistent improvement in model performance
